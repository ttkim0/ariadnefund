Thesis

The labyrinth that Theseus entered had walls but no map. Ariadne gave him a thread — a continuous, calibrated reference back to the entrance. Without it he would not have found his way out, no matter how cleverly he searched. The myth is, among other things, an early argument for probabilistic reasoning under uncertainty: a single observation tells you almost nothing, but a trail of consistent observations tells you everything.

Prediction markets — Kalshi, Polymarket, and the event-contract exchanges that follow them — are the modern labyrinth. Liquidity is uneven. Settlement triggers vary. Information arrives at odd intervals. The participant mix is mostly retail, which leaves durable but small inefficiencies in the price-discovery process. Most participants navigate these markets by guessing the outcome. We navigate them by holding a calibrated thread — a probabilistic distribution we can read back at every step, and a set of decision rules that act only where the thread tells us the market is mispriced.

A typical retail participant sees a contract chain of mutually- exclusive outcomes — six temperature buckets, a dozen NFL score lines, the candidates in a primary — and asks which one will it be? They make a guess, possibly check a forecast or a poll, and place a bet. We do not do that. The model does not pick a bucket at all: it outputs a calibrated probability for every outcome simultaneously, and the trading layer compares each of those probabilities to the market mid. Nine times out of ten the comparison favors the market and we hold no position. The tenth time, the gap is real, and we trade it with size proportional to its statistical significance.

Approach

The system, end-to-end, is four steps in series. None of them is novel on its own; the value comes from doing all four with discipline and from refusing to act when any one of them tells us not to.

One. Forecast distributions, not point estimates. We train multi-quantile gradient-boosting models — one per forecast horizon and one per quantile, $7 \times 7 = 49$ models for the hourly grid alone — on station-level historical data going back to 1970. The output of the model is not a single predicted temperature but a calibrated cumulative distribution function over the settlement quantity, with empirical coverage on the validation set within two percentage points of nominal across all seven trained quantiles.

Two. Map distributions onto contracts. Each Kalshi strike is a binary contract with a precise settlement rule against the National Weather Service Climatological Report. We convert our predicted CDF into a per-strike YES probability via cumulative- distribution interpolation that respects the exchange's integer-rounding convention. Quantile crossing is corrected by cumulative-max monotonization before interpolation.

Three. Calibrate against the market. A small logistic-plus-isotonic meta-calibrator blends our model probability with the Kalshi market mid. The blend learns where each signal is more informative than the other — typically our model dominates inside twelve hours of settlement and the market dominates outside it. On a held-out April 2026 slice the meta-blend's log-loss is $0.376$ versus the market's $0.469$, a $19.8\%$ skill score.

Four. Trade only when post-fee EV exceeds threshold. Fractional Kelly sizing capped at five percent of capital per trade, a $0.02 minimum EV, hard caps on per-market exposure at half of the candle-time open interest, and a slippage model proportional to size beyond the first one hundred contracts. The discipline of declining bad opportunities is as much of the edge as identifying good ones.

The exploitable inefficiency in this asset class sits at the join of forecast post-processing and market microstructure rather than at either alone. — Paper 01, §8 Discussion

The asymmetry of the win-rate split — sixty-five percent at twelve hours, seventy-eight percent at six — is not a quirk. It is the single most important fact about this strategy. It says that our edge is not a forecasting edge in the meteorological sense. It is the speed at which we integrate the most recent station observations into a calibrated probability, relative to how quickly the market participants do the same. As the time-to-settlement compresses, the day's true extreme becomes more and more determined by past observations and less and less by future weather. The market converges toward the right answer slowly. Our model converges faster.

What we trade, and what we don't

We trade liquid event-contract chains where the underlying truth source is observable shortly before settlement. Daily-temperature contracts at U.S. airport stations are the cleanest example, and where Strategy 01 lives today. The National Weather Service publishes the official daily high and low for each station to integer precision. The contract chain is six mutually-exclusive 2°F buckets. The settlement source is a public auditable record. The participant mix is heavily retail. All four conditions for a tradable inefficiency are satisfied. The same four conditions hold for a much wider universe of contracts — box scores in progress, electricity-demand auctions, monthly economic releases, airline on-time statistics — and the framework generalizes directly.

We do not trade at horizons where we have no informational advantage. For weather at twenty-four hours and beyond, the market participants have access to numerical weather prediction model output — GFS, HRRR, ECMWF — whose forecast skill our station-only model cannot match. The system simply abstains in that regime. The same logic applies elsewhere: we do not bet on outcomes that depend on information we are not privileged to read faster than the crowd. Discipline about when not to trade is the part of the strategy that most retail participants get wrong and that most professional managers get right. We get it right by mechanical rule rather than by judgment.

We do not trade contracts whose settlement source is opaque, delayed, or subject to discretion. That excludes most novelty contracts, headline-political contracts where the settlement reference is itself an opinion poll, and any contract whose resolution depends on an exchange's editorial judgment rather than a public auditable record.

Roadmap

Weather is the first domain because it has the cleanest settlement mechanics and the most transparent observational record — an ideal training ground for the calibration discipline the strategy depends on. The methodology generalizes far beyond it. Each new strategy follows the same template: identify a contract universe whose ground truth is publicly observable, build a model that outputs a calibrated distribution over outcomes, and trade only the gaps between that distribution and the market.

Strategy 02 (sports). Live in-game NFL, NBA, and MLB markets on Kalshi and Polymarket, where line movement lags the play-by-play feed by a measurable interval. The same quantile-forecasting machinery that produces a CDF over a daily temperature produces a CDF over a final score given the current game state.

Strategy 03 (macro releases). CPI, non-farm payrolls, FOMC outcomes, GDP advance estimates — contracts that settle on a single scheduled government release. The market typically prices these against consensus economist surveys; we price them against a distributional model trained on the underlying high-frequency data, with explicit handling of the revision and seasonal-adjustment processes that introduce most of the residual uncertainty.

Strategy 04 (energy and grid). Day-ahead and intra-day electricity demand and price contracts on regional ISO data, where the settlement source is a public ISO data feed and the underlying load follows weather and calendar drivers we already model.

We are deliberate about expansion. Each new strategy must clear the same statistical bar Strategy 01 cleared on a held-out test set before it is allocated capital. Building a calibrated thread for a new asset class is not an afternoon's work, and we treat it that way.
